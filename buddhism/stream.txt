In the Śrāvaka practices, including  Theravāda Buddhism, a srotaāpanna (or stream-enterer) is an individual who has reached the first stage of enlightenment and achieved non-retrogression towards becoming an Arhat. This means they may struggle but not never regress towards enlightenment.. This attainment is characterized by a profound shift in consciousness, marked by the irreversible elimination of three primary fetters (saṃyojana): belief in a permanent self (sakkāya-diṭṭhi), doubt in the teachings (vicikicchā), and clinging to rites and rituals as ends in themselves (sīlabbata-parāmāsa). A stream-enterer is thus assured of reaching Nirvāṇa within a maximum of seven rebirths and will no longer be reborn in the lower realms (animal, ghost, or hell realms). This state is celebrated for its liberation from certain delusions, which sets a practitioner on a clear, irreversible path toward full awakening. 
In the Bodhisattva tradition, particularly as expressed in Mahāyāna Buddhism, the concept of srotaāpanna is often adapted to align with the broader goal of attaining Buddhahood rather than achieving only Arhatship. They also have the concept of those fetters dropping and they have a concept of non-retrogression.
